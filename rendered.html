[COMS W3157] Advanced Programming

Nov 2, 2023
TLDR: I hated pretty much everything about this class, it dried up any passion I had for learning CS, made me lose hope as a student at Columbia, and pushed me to seek therapy. I wish I could say that sentence was a joke or an exaggeration, but it was the literal truth. I switched to a different major after this course. This is not because this class is a weed-out (every one passes if you don’t cheat). Rather, it’s the soul-sucking, inhumane, pedagogical vileness and borderline insanity that occupies the mind of one Jae Woo Lee that pushed me away from this course and major, and he’s a nice guy too so imagine that. More comprehensive review: Jae's philosophy is essentially: if you try, you'll learn a lot. What this does not mean: 1) you will do well on exams 2) you will do well on labs 3) content will click immediately. In fact, it's quite the opposite. It is through the grueling intensity of this course's homework and the monumental challenges that are the exams that the fundamental material of this course will be cemented into your brain by the end of it (provided you don't cheat and pay attention). You will understand how a computer works (at the surface level at least); you will be able to work with a large pre-written codebase; you will be prepared for higher level systems and database courses. These are the central assertions that Jae makes in the first lecture and then rigorously proves over the course. However, will you end up liking CS after this course? For many, I don't think so. Understanding and learning a lot does not equate to enjoyment. The massive influx of knowledge you get from this course comes at the cost of your passion for CS drying up. Jae will contest this. In fact, the paper he published (and continues to promote) about this course has (carefully selected) reviews of students who took his course and developed a newfound passion for CS that they did not get from data structures and 1004/1006/1002. One example includes a physics student who switched to CS after taking AP and then ended up getting a PhD in the subject from a top university. This entire paper is predicated on flawed reasoning (pretty typical for STEM professors attempting sociological research): that people study computer science because they like computer science. Most don't. Most saw TikToks about the 150k+ salary that software engineers make and decided to enroll in Intro-to-Java. Most see the degree as a means to an end (a job). A minority probably like systems as a subject (they'll end up taking OS or becoming TAs), but do not *DELUDE* yourself that this course is going to make you like CS if you view this degree as a path to an engineering job at some tech company. When this course is over, you will be relieved, so relieved as to the point that you might elicit a tear or two that it’s finally over. You survived the war. The finish-line. You made it. Then, whether or not you have a brain, you’ll either take more courses with Jae or move onto better professors. Is C + UNIX programming useful? No! Jae admits this in lecture 1. What you actually learn from this course is less about C and more about a) how a computer works and the internal memory processes behind it, b) the basics of OS, and c) how to practice iterative development. Teaching C and UNIX is simply a vessel to that end. Some niche advice: Don't fall into the cult of personality surrounding Jae. He's a nice guy, but he isn't divine when it comes to CS education. He will try to justify AP’s structure and workload off of a paper he wrote in 2011; he will cite articles on his webpage about how Bwog considers AP one of the best classes at Columbia; he will crack a joke or two about his past life as a developer. Do not fall into this trap that you must take more courses with him. LEAVE. As that guy in Get Out said, GET OUT! Here are some immediate red flags that should tell you to leave: - In the first lecture, he led with “you’ve probably read some bad reviews about me.” - “You might not do well even if you try hard” - a quote from his syllabus powerpoint. - He beefed with Hillary Clinton over some blackboards. Remember the last guy who publicly beefed with Hillary Clinton? Look what happened to him. - He continuously has to justify the difficulty of his course (mostly after every exam or an exceptionally hard homework) by citing papers he wrote, carefully selected student reviews. - About 15 minutes before the second midterm, he emailed the entire class listserv a screenshot of text messages with his OWN SON who was taking AP as well. In these messages, his son was lamenting about how hard one of the practice exams were. After this screenshot, Jae wrote a “good luck” message and signed off on the email. Diving into the thought process by this psychotic action, he probably wanted to convey “hey even my own son thinks my course is hard. Don’t worry about that much.” What it ended up coming off as was “hah lol my son is struggling with exams I WROTE lemme expose him to a group of 650 classmates.” This course is a hurdle that every CS major has to get through. Climb, fumble, collapse, or jump effortlessly over this hurdle and do not look back! On the bright side, I know entire friend groups that formed during AP over the sheer collective trauma. You will leave this course with some new comrades to swap war stories with over a couple of cigars. You’ll never see CS majors be more social than when the AP Lab is due. To finish off this review, I'll leave a quote from his first lecture powerpoint: "Please don’t get stressed out about AP." I did.

Workload
- 7 homework assignments called labs which take north of 10 hours to complete on average. Your lowest lab is dropped and the other 6 are compiled to 25% of your overall grade. - 3 exams worth 25% of your grade each. These are hard, tricky, annoying, dumb, intuitive, useless, and a slew of other adjectives. You almost always don't have enough time to do them. Averages vary. Exams are graded and returned very quickly. - This course is very tightly scheduled and has an exorbitantly fast pace. If you fall behind by more than 2 weeks, consider P/Fing the course or dropping it entirely. It is extremely difficult to catch up. Don't take this course with other hard courses. Try to lighten your load the semester you plan to take this class. It is not for the weak-willed and the faint of heart. There will be many tears. There will be much sorrow. Many will tell you this course “isn’t as bad as people think.” I implore you to look up the definition of survivorship bias. Lecture notes are uploaded to the webpage, so don’t feel compelled to take copious notes. Focus on understanding. This starts at lecture 1 and does not end until the lecture before the final. I barely went to OH though. The TAs in this course definitely know what they're talking about content-wise but they're actual advice for doing well in this course is subpar at best. For example, a TA emailed us a practice exam problem, citing that we should be able to complete it in under 50 minutes. During the practice problem review session, it took the TA 1 hour and 20 minutes to complete the problem. Moreover, there was repetitive emphasis on "drawing it out" which I found to be garbage advice. For one, you only get 75 minutes for two-three fairly difficult questions. Spending time to draw everything out is wasting precious time to actually get to the right answer.

I stepped into 3157 with the same fear as everyone else. I walked out with a stack of knowledge, countless tricks, and... still a lingering fear. It was an intense course, but with the right approach, it’s more than manageable. Content: Yes. Jae is *that* good. You've probably heard some horror stories—and I can confirm them—but his course design and lectures are truly exceptional. In the end, - You will learn a lot about C - You will build a solid foundation in Git and UNIX - You will be a very careful coder, thanks to his harsh grading :( Are these useful? Not always, but think of it as a workout on your programming habit and attitude. ---------- Survival tips - Attend lectures in-person, and take good notes - Join Section 2 for extra help—it's where Jae shows his nice side (yes, it exists) - Do the practice exams and review solutions carefully - Start the labs EARLY. Debugging under pressure is not a fun sport. - The TAs and ListServ are incredibly helpful!

Workload
TLDR: "Grades may not be super accurate in terms of assessing your understanding." – Jae Lee, Day 1 Grading is HARSH and often binary. Just try your best. ---------- Assignments: 7 assignments (called labs), each with clear guidelines - Lectures often hint at the general approach to a solution - No crazy programming gymnastics needed—if you need something, he'll give you or tell you where to look. - Labs 6 and 7 are significantly longer but build on earlier labs - Debugging is where the real time goes. The rubric is harsh, so precision matters. - As always, start the labs EARLY. Not because they take forever, but reinforcing concepts early pays off in this fast-paced class. ---------- Exams: Jae is an awesome exam writer, testing your understanding to the finest detail. - The problems aren't impossible–everything comes directly from the lectures and labs. The real challenge lies in managing stress and focusing on meticulous details. - Some problems offer generous partial credit. Others? Miss one part and the others crash down like an avalanche ... - Practice helps, but luck plays a role too. At the end of the day, we are human, not computers, not gcc -g -Wall. We make mistakes, and that’s part of what makes us human. ---------- Grading His grading system is statistically fair for a class with 400 students: on AVERAGE, good students do well, and bad students struggle. However, each of us only takes 3 exams, not 400, so this "fairness" may break down entirely on an individual scale. The takeaway is: Always try your best, but never over-stress about your AP grade. It's your learning and improvement that matter. Your grade is just a number; it correlates with, but doesn’t always define, your abilities. Also don’t lose sleep over an A+. It’s reserved for the top 3-ish unfortunately...

You’ll love Jae sometimes, you’ll hate him other times, but most of all you’ll get why every Columbia student knows his name. In spite of everything, I still recommend him over Borowski unless you know all this stuff already or have a really rough semester scheduled. Unless you really care about having > 4.0 (rather than, let’s say, a 3.95) or can’t code in intro to Java/data structures without ChatGPT, there are no downsides to this class.

I was really afraid of taking Advanced Programming due to the fearmongering, so much so that I dropped a course just so that I could focus on AP. One important thing to note is that we are in Columbia University, where people start tweaking out over an A-, meaning that a good chunk of the comments about courses can sometimes be blown out of proportion. That being said, while AP is definitely not an easy class, I would not really rate it as impossible, considering a lot of people do fairly well. I agree with most reviews here that Jae is a great lecturer and he can explain concepts very easily in his lectures. As for the content of the class, on the surface level, while the majority of the ideas taught in class are straightforward, they are prone to becoming really tricky really fast. In other words, you are expected to 100% understand a concept in order to do well in the exams. This is a really demanding class, and people do well only if they put in the effort.

Workload
7 labs (coding assignments), 3 exams. A total of 7 late days were given for the labs. The first five labs are graded out of 100, while lab 6 is graded out of 120 pts, and lab 7 is graded out of 150. The labs build upon one another, as does the content of the course, so it is important you understand what the prominent functions in each labs do. The first 5 labs will probably take around 5 hours to complete on average. Lab 6 and 7 take SIGNIFICANTLY longer. Grading is kind of harsh what can I say. The exams are, as Jae claims, really tricky sometimes. It is like preparing for a standardized exam. Which sucks imo. Just prepare well and get on that analytical mindset.
